# Position Sizing

Position sizing answers one of the most important questions in trading: **"How much money should I put into this trade?"**

Getting this wrong is the number one reason beginners blow up their accounts. Too big and one bad trade ruins you. Too small and your winners do not matter. The goal is to find the sweet spot where you can survive a losing streak while still growing your account.

## The Key Idea

Every trade has a point where you admit you were wrong — that is your **stop loss**. Position sizing calculates how many units to trade so that _if_ you hit your stop loss, you only lose a small, predetermined percentage of your account.

> [!FOREX]
> **Pip**: The smallest standard price movement in forex. For most pairs like EUR/USD, one pip is 0.0001 (the fourth decimal place). If EUR/USD moves from 1.0850 to 1.0860, it moved 10 pips.

> [!FOREX]
> **Unit**: The smallest quantity of currency you can trade. Think of it like shares in the stock market — one unit is one "piece" of currency. Traders also talk about **lots**: a standard lot is 100,000 units, a mini lot is 10,000, and a micro lot is 1,000.

## The 1% Rule

Most professional traders follow a simple guideline:

**Never risk more than 1–2% of your account balance on a single trade.**

Here is why this works:

| Account Size | 1% Risk | What This Means                            |
| ------------ | ------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| $5,000       | $50     | If the trade hits stop loss, you lose $50  |
| $10,000      | $100    | If the trade hits stop loss, you lose $100 |
| $25,000      | $250    | If the trade hits stop loss, you lose $250 |

Even if you lose 10 trades in a row at 1% risk (which does happen), you have only lost about 10% of your account. That is recoverable. But 10 trades at 10% risk each? That is a 65% loss — devastating and extremely hard to come back from.

> [!WARNING]
> Beginners are often tempted to risk 5% or even 10% per trade to "make money faster." This almost always leads to a blown account. Start with 1% and increase only after months of consistent profitability.

## Using the Position Sizer Tool

FXFlow has a built-in Position Sizer to do the maths for you. Here is how to use it:

### Step 1: Enter Your Details

- **Account balance**: Your current account value (e.g., $10,000)
- **Risk percentage**: How much you want to risk (e.g., 1%)
- **Stop loss distance**: How many pips away your stop loss is from your entry price

### Step 2: Read the Result

The tool calculates the exact number of **units** to trade. For example:

> Account: $10,000 | Risk: 1% ($100) | Stop loss: 25 pips
> Result: Trade **40,000 units** (0.4 standard lots)

This means if price moves 25 pips against you, you lose exactly $100 — which is 1% of your account.

### Step 3: Use That Number

When placing your trade in FXFlow, enter the unit size the calculator gives you. Do not round up "because the trade looks really good." The whole point is consistency.

> [!TIP]
> Bookmark the Position Sizer. Use it before _every single trade_. After a while it becomes second nature, but never skip it.

## Why This Matters So Much

### Losing Streaks Are Normal

Even great traders with a 60% win rate will sometimes lose 5, 6, or 7 trades in a row. This is completely normal — it is just how probability works. Position sizing ensures that a losing streak is an inconvenience, not a catastrophe.

### Your Edge Works Over Many Trades

A trading strategy does not work on every single trade. It works over hundreds of trades, where the wins slightly outweigh the losses. If you blow up your account after 20 trades because you sized too big, you never gave your strategy a chance to play out.

### It Removes Emotion

When your risk is small and predefined, losing a trade is not a big deal. You shrug it off and move to the next one. When your risk is huge, every trade becomes stressful, and stress leads to terrible decisions.

> [!NOTE]
> The position sizer accounts for different pip values across currency pairs. A pip on EUR/USD is worth a different dollar amount than a pip on GBP/JPY. The tool handles this automatically — you do not need to calculate it yourself.
